 Introduction (1/3)
Eye cancers are tumorous growths found in the ocular region of the head.

Eye Cancers have been known to spread to other parts of the body relatively quickly when compared to other diseases, something which makes them difficult to treat effectively.

There are three primary types of Eye Cancer: Intraocular Melanomas, Intraocular Lymphomas, and Retinoblastomas. The specifics of each type will be discussed thouroughly down below.
Introduction (2/3)
While Eye Cancers can impact anyone, they are especially prominent in males over the age of 55.

Eye Cancers actually account for anywhere from 5 to 7.5 million unique cases of cancer throughout the US and Europe.

To put this in perspective, over 2,500 adults each year in the US alone will be diagnosed with this terrible disease.

Types of Cancer (1/2)
Retinoblastomas
 Retinoblastomas are a form of Eye Cancer that is found exclusively in children under the age of 5. Although Retinoblastomas are by no means common, their influence demands attention.

They are usually contracted as a result of a mutation in a gene referred to in the scientific community as the RB gene.

Types of Cancer (2/2)
Intraocular Melanomas
 Before we can properly dicuss Intraocular Melanomas and the impact that they have on people, it's important to first understand exactly what Melanoma is.

In short, Melanoma is caused when malignant cells rapidly grow from melanin, a pigment in the skin which is responsible for tanning. This explains why there are health restrictions on tanning beds, and why you use sunscream when you go to the beach.

Medical Advancements (1/2)
Radiation Therapy
 Radiation therapy makes use of high energy x-rays or other types of radiation to kill cancer cells. It is a common treatment for many forms of Eye Cancer, but is used most commonly to treat Intraocular Melanoma.

While radiation can often preserve some vision in the eye, this sometimes may be lost anyway if the radiation damages vital structures near the infected point.

Medical Advancements (2/2)
Ocular Surgery
 Although its application has been limited in recent years in favor of radiation therapy, surgery is sometimes used to treat certain forms of Eye cancers.

The exception to this is Intraocular Lymphomas, where surgery is not an option due to the fact that the disease has almost certainly spread beyond the point of origin before such options are explored.

How to Help (1/2)
Eye Tumor Research Foundation
 The Eye Tumor Research Foundation is a foundation devoted to research in eye cancers in an effort to extend life and sight and improve patient care by developing and improving methods of diagnosis and treatments.

The contributions made to the foundation are specifically for one purpose—to provide support for research in the investigation of eye cancers.

 Established in 1998 as The EyeCare Foundation,The Eye Cancer Foundation focuses on eye cancer and are an educational and supportive resource for eye cancer patients, their families, and physicians.
